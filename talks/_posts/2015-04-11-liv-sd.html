Introduction
Affective States

Introduction

  • Research suggests that teachers’ affective states are closely linked to success in professional development movements (Schmidt & Datnow, 2005; Van Driel, Beijaard, & Verloop, 2011).
  • Developed as part of a project about Saudi Arabian teachers’ affective states towards reforms in science education.
  • Three aspects were investigated in the project. Science teachers’
    • pedagogical discontentment (STPD),
    • self-efficacy (STEBI), and
    • intentions to reform (IRSTS).
kahveci.pw/dgq

Conceptualisation of Pedagogical Discontentment

  • Pedagogical discontentment means
    • teachers’ assessment of contextual aspects, and
    • a state of cognitive conflict.
  • High levels of pedagogical discontentment might be interpreted as an important precursor to change.

Theoretical Framework

Development of the Original STPD Scale (Southerland et al., 2012)

kahveci.pw/dgq

Purpose

•

To explore the measurement integrity of the scores on the Science Teachers’ Pedagogical Discontentment (STPD) scale in the Saudi Arabia context

Research Questions

•

What structure underlies responses to the measure

—that is, does the (Arabic) structure correspond to the original scale? (Reflecting the validity issue)

•

Are scores on the STPD scale as a whole and as per each factor category reliable? (Reflecting the reliability issue)

Method

•

This is a survey research designed to investigate science teachers’ pedagogical discontentment in Saudi Arabia (Jaeger, 1988)

•

Conducting a factor analysis of the observed scores on a given instrument, one can determine if the test is measuring the variables it purports to, which is the definition of construct validation (Stapleton, 1997)

Cross-Cultural Validation

•

Attending to Sperber et al.’s (1994) cautions and suggestions, the original instrument was first translated into Arabic by a bilingual science educator fluent in both English and Arabic languages.

•

The Arabic version of the instrument was back- translated to English by independent science educators in Saudi Arabia also fluent in English.

•

The Saudi science educators did not see the original English instrument.

Item Revision for a Better Match to the Arabic Context

•

Some of the items were revised and clarified to respond to the Saudi Arabia context. For example;

  • “balancing personal science teaching goals with those of state and national standards”
  • “balancing personal science teaching goals with those requirements, standards, goals set out by the Ministry of Education”

One Item Omitted

•

“balancing personal science teaching goals with state/national testing requirements” was omitted from the STPD scale because the Ministry of Education has not released any testing benchmarks or standards.

The Final Stage of Development

•

The STPD instrument that eventually included 20 items was evaluated by seven Saudi science teachers specialising in physics, chemistry, biology, and geology, in terms of suitability for the local population of teachers.

•

Their feedback was sought to identify any ambiguities or difficulties.

Sample & Demographics

  • A total of 994 science teachers
    • 656 females and 338 males
    • teaching physics (N=90, 9.1%), chemistry (N=93, 9.4%), biology (N=56, 5.6%) at secondary school level
    • general science (N=682, 68.6%) at elementary and middle school levels

    • teaching experience: 36.6% participants fall into their first 5 years, 26.2% up to 10 years, 15.5% up to 15

      years, 13.4% up to 20 years, and 9.3% 25 or more years

Data Check & Cleaning

•

All of the items were evaluated in terms of their goodness of fit for the normality condition.

•

The main indicator in this stage was the identification of cases, those standard scores of which exceed +/-3 boundary condition.

•

Scores were calculated in SPSS’s list-wise selection, resulting in 982 valid cases. Thus, 12 cases were omitted from further analyses at this stage allowing one to employ advanced parametric test.

Step 1. Confirmatory Factor Analysis (Main Data, N=982)

  • The CFA was run over IBM AMOS 21 with the following analysis properties:
    • Discrepancy: Maximum likelihood
    • Fit measures with incomplete data: Fit the saturated and independence model
    • Output: Standardized estimates, Residual moments, Modification indices

Step 1: Conclusion

•

As chi-square per degree of freedom exceeds the threshold value (>5), and similarly GFI, AGFI, RMSEA and PCLOSE values are above the respective threshold values, one would conclude that the suggested model is not supported by the data.

•

This analysis suggest that Arabic STPD instrument has a different factor structure and therefore the data is randomly split in half and so that EFA and CFA would be re-run over the dataset.

Step 2. Exploratory Factor Analysis (Split-Half, N= 471 )

  • The EFA was run over IBM SPSS 19
  • The KMO index yielding .974 > .05 suggests that the correlation matrix is not an identity matrix and sampling is suitable to run the factor analysis
  • the Bartlett’s Test of Sphericity (p=.000<.050) confirms that there is a high correlation among the items, and thus, factor analysis should reliably discern the patterns over the dataset (Kaiser, 1974).

Step 3: Conclusion

•

As chi-square per degree of freedom is in the acceptable region (<5), and similarly CFI, GFI, AGFI, and RMSEA values are under the threshold values, one would conclude that the suggested one-factor model is a good fit.

•

Thus, Arabic STPD instrument is a one-dimensional instrument measuring one construct, named STPD.

•

With respect to treatments to suggested modification indices, many of the highly loaded ones are applied because there is only one latent variable and observed variables predicting common latent variables could share error variances.

Discussion

•

Having only one component, namely “Science teachers’ pedagogical discontentment (STPD)” indicates that the teachers in Saudi Arabia perceive their pedagogical discontentment as one-dimensional mismatch with their pedagogical goals and teaching practice.

•

The potential reason for one-dimensional result could be that Arabic science teachers might have less experience in professional development towards alternative teaching methods such as inquiry-based science education as opposed to their American counterparts.

•

This reason might reveal broader understandings about reforms and their effects in Saudi Arabia context.

Discussion

•

This evidence is alignment with the current debate in Saudi Arabia, indeed.

•

Although there has been a shift from traditional teaching to inquiry-based science education, Almazroa and Alorini, as cited by Almazroa (2013) claim that professional development activities that are offered to teachers do not meet the demand of new curriculum.

Conclusion

•

The results of this study confirm that the Arabic version of the STPD scale is valid and highly reliable.

•

It can be used in this specific context as a one- dimensional affective state to measure teachers’ pedagogical discontentment towards teaching science.
