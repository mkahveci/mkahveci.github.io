Affective Dimensions in Chemistry Education

An Overview of the Edited Volume

Murat Kahveci, Editor
Canakkale Onsekiz Mart University, Turkey 		MaryKay Orgill, Editor
University of Nevada, Las Vegas, USA

Published 2015

--- ## The Problem: An Imbalance in Education * For decades, educational objectives have been categorized into three domains: **cognitive, affective, and psychomotor** (Bloom et al. 1956, 1964). * The **cognitive domain** has received significantly more attention from researchers, particularly in chemistry education. * This has led to a global issue where, despite an increase in university enrollment, the proportion of students choosing science fields is **decreasing**. * International studies like PISA and the EU's "Rocard Report" highlight this alarming trend. -- ### The Consequences of Neglecting Affect
Region Observation
USA (1995 vs. 2003) A 12% drop in physical science graduates (from ~100,000 to ~88,000).
Germany (1995 vs. 2003) A dramatic 50% loss in physical science graduates (from ~101,000 to ~50,000).
USA & EU27 (2005) Females comprised only about 31% of graduates in mathematics, science, and technology.

A focus on affective dimensions is crucial to address this global lack of interest and retention in science.

--- ## What Are Affective Dimensions? The affective domain refers to the complex constellation of psychological constructs that influence learning. This includes a student's: * Attitudes * Values * Beliefs * Opinions * Emotions * Interests * Motivation * Degree of acceptance or rejection

(Krathwohl, Bloom, & Masia, 1964)

-- ### Why Is Affect So Often Ignored? 1. **Measurement is Challenging:** Affective constructs are not easily observed, and students may mask their true feelings to get credit for "valued behaviors". 2. **Perception of Science:** Many scientists and educators present science as purely objective and reason-driven, separate from subjective feelings and values. 3. **Assessment Focus:** Educational systems worldwide prioritize assessing cognitive outcomes because it is perceived to be easier than measuring affective ones. --- ## Goal of This Volume This book was created to gather the most up-to-date expertise and research about the influence of the **affective domain on learning in chemistry** into a single, accessible resource. We hope this volume will: * Serve as a foundational resource for researchers and practitioners. * Inspire greater consideration of the affective domain in both chemistry teaching and future education research. -- ### Structure of the Book The book is organized into two distinct parts that bridge the gap between foundational ideas and practical application. * **Part I: Theoretical Considerations** * Reviews the core theories related to the influence of the affective domain on chemistry learning. * **Part II: Research and Practice** * Connects research findings to the practical challenges of teaching and learning chemistry. --- ## Part I: Theoretical Considerations This section establishes the conceptual groundwork for understanding affect in chemistry education. **Key themes include:** * Constructivist ideas and their influence on affective objectives (Taber). * Methods for evaluating affective dimensions (Rahayu). * Theories of motivation and interest, and the role of context-based learning (Menthe & Parchmann). * The impact of gender on student affect in chemistry (A. Kahveci). * The role of intuitive beliefs in teaching socio-scientific issues (Dittmer & Gebhard). --- ## Part II: Research and Practice This section presents a global collection of studies and practical applications. **Key themes include:** * Fostering engagement for special-needs students through inquiry (Abels). * Meeting the affective and cognitive needs of gifted learners (Taber). * Evaluating affect in context-based learning environments (Fechner et al.). * Cross-cultural validation of affective instruments (Xu et al.). * Chemistry self-efficacy in secondary schools (Cheung). * Problem-based learning labs and their influence on student attitudes (Yoon et al.). * The potential of cognitive neuroscience methods in affective research (Liu & Huang). * Using drawings to explore teachers' pedagogical attitudes (Markic & Eilks). * Teacher attitudes towards linguistic heterogeneity (Markic). * Gender-based affective states toward learning physical chemistry (M. Kahveci). --- ## Conclusion * The historic focus on the cognitive domain has coincided with declining student interest and retention in the sciences. * Positive affective dimensions—like interest, motivation, and positive attitudes—are strongly correlated with student persistence and performance. * This volume provides a comprehensive foundation of theory, research, and practice to empower educators and researchers to better address the **affective dimensions in chemistry education**. --- ## Sourcing Information * This presentation was synthesized from the preface and table of contents of the following academic book: * Kahveci, M., & Orgill, M.
